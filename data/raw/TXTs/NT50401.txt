STATEMENT 
Exchange Bancorporation, Inc., Tampa, Florida ("Applicant"), 
has filed with the Board, pursuant to section 3(a)(J) of the Bank Holding 
Company Act of 1956, an application for approval of action to become a 
bank holding company through the acquisition of 80 per cent or more of 
the voting shares of The Exchange National Bank of Tampa, Tampa 
("Exchange Tampa"), The Exchange Bank of Temple Terrace, Temple Terrace 
("Exchange Temple Terrace"), and Exchange National Bank of Winter Haven, 
Winter Haven ("Exchange Winter Haven"), hereinafter sometimes jointly 
referred to as "The Exchange Banks"; and 60 per cent or more of the 
voting shares of Gulf-to-Bay Bank & Trust Company, Clearwater ("Gulf-to-
Bay"), all in the State of Florida. 
Both Exchange Tampa and Exchange Temple Terrace are in 
Hillsborough County, at the north end of Tampa Bay on the west coast 
of Florida. Exchange Tampa ($164.7 million deposits)-^ is located in 
the downtown area of Tampa, and Exchange Temple Terrace ($7.5 million 
deposits) is located in the suburban city of Temple Terrace, about 10 
miles northwest of downtown Tampa.

Exchange Winter Haven ($42.7 million deposits) is located in

Statutory considerations. - Section 3(c) of the Act provides 
that the Board shall not approve an acquisition that would result in a 
monopoly or would be in furtherance of any combination or conspiracy to 
monopolize or to attempt to monopolize the business of banking in any 
part of the United States. Nor may the Board approve a proposed 
acquisition the effect of which, in any section of the country, may be 
substantially to lessen competition, or to tend to create a monopoly, or 
which in any other manner would be in restraint of trade, unless the 
Board finds that the anticompetitive effects of the proposed transaction 
are clearly outweighed in the public interest by the probable effect of 
the transaction in meeting the convenience and needs of the community to 
be served. In each case the Board is required to take into consideration 
the financial and managerial resources and future prospects of the bank 
holding company and the banks concerned, and the convenience and needs 
of the community to be served.

Competitive effect: of proposed transaction. -Â» Consummation 
of the proposed transaction would result in Applicant's becoming the 
sixth largest banking organization and bank holding company in the 
State of Florida. The $233 million aggregate deposits held by its 
proposed subsidiaries represent 2.3 per cent of the total deposits held 
by banks in the State. 
Of the proposed subsidiary banks, only Exchange Tampa and 
Exchange Temple Terrace appear to be located within sufficient proximity 
to each other to be significant competitors. In fact, however, competition 
between any of the four banks is negligible. Exchange Temple Terrace 
and Exchange Winter Haven both were organized by the management and 
principal stockholders of Exchange Tampa, and have been closely and 
continuously affiliated with Exchange Tampa since their organization. 
Shareholders who own 61 per cent of the stock of Exchange Tampa also 
own about the same percentage of the stock of Exchange Winter Haven, and 
a majority of the stock of Exchange Temple Terrace is owned by a 
corporation, all of the stock of which is held by trustees for the benefit 
of Exchange Tampa's shareholders. Gulf-to-Bay, the only one of the four 
banks which is not a party to the existing affiliation, is located 
20 miles from Exchange Tampa, the nearest of the Exchange Banks. It does 
not Compete with any of the Exchange Banks and, in view of a State law 
which prohibits branching, such competition is unlikely to develop in 
the future.

It is therefore reasonably anticipated that approval of the 
application would have no significant present or future effect on 
competition between the proposed subsidiaries.. 
The principal area served by Exchange Tampa is the City of 
Tampa. It is the second largest bank in Tampa and competes principally 
with two other large downtown Tampa banks and with several neighborhood 
banks in the city. Exchange Temple Terrace primarily serves the 
northeastern section of the city and competes principally with three 
larger banks located near that area. Exchange Winter Haven, the second 
largest bank in Polk County, serves the Winter Haven-Cypress Gardens 
area of the county, and competes principally with three other area banks, 
including two subsidiaries of Barnett National Securities Corporation, 
a registered bank holding company. Gulf-to-Bay ranks twentieth in size 
among Pinellas County banks, and is the fourth largest of seven banks 
in Clearwater. It appears that, in view of the present affiliation among 
the Exchange Banks, the only bank whose competitive ability would be 
significantly strengthened as a result of consummation of the proposed 
transaction is Gulf-to-Bay. Competition in the Clearwater area should be 
increased as a result, and it does not appear that the viability or 
competitive effectiveness of competing banks in any area would bo adversely 
affected to any undue extent. 
In view of the foregoing, the Board concludes that the proposed 
transaction would not result in a monopoly or be in furtherance of any

combination, conspiracy or attempt to monopolize the business of banking 
in any relevant area, nor would such consummation restrain trade, tend 
to create a monopoly, or substantially lessen competition in any section 
of the country. 
Financial and managerial resources and future prospects. -
Applicant is a newly formed corporation and has no financial or operating 
history. Its financial condition, managerial resources, and prospects 
would be entirely dependent upon those of the subsidiary banks. 
The four proposed subsidiaries are located in prosperous, 
Populous, and rapidly growing areas in west-central Florida. Their 
financial conditions, managerial resources and prospects are considered 
to be generally satisfactory; Gulf-to-Bay, however, would likely benefit 
from the strengthened management which Applicant could provide and the 
increased facility of the holding company for raising additional capital. 
The Board concludes, therefore, that considerations relating to the 
banking factors are consistent with approval of the application, and lend 
some weight in support thereof as they relate to Gulf-to-Bay. 
Convenience and needs of the communities involved. - The banking 
needs of the three counties in which the proposed subsidiaries are 
located are being adequately served at the present time. 
No changes are proposed in the services offered by the Exchange 
Banks. It is anticipated that approval of the application would result 
in improvement and expansion of Gulf-to.-fiay's small trust department 
through the assistance of the trust staff and facilities of Exchange 

Tampa, and that Gulf-to-Bay would be better able to serve the borrowing 
needs of customers in its area through expanded lending limits and the 
specialized lending expertise of Exchange Tampa's staff. A more 
sophisticated accounting system for the subsidiary banks is proposed 
and also a personnel training and recruiting program, both of which 
could indirectly result in improved service to the public. 
Considerations under this factor provide some additional weight 
in favor of approval of the application. 
Summary and conclusion. - On the basis of all relevant facts 
contained in the record, and in the light of the factors set forth in 
section 3(c) of the Act, it is the Board's judgment that the proposed 
transaction would be in the public interest and that the application 
should be approved.

Dear Mr. Chairman: 
This is in response to your letter of January 21, 1969, 
raising questions concerning the Board's regulation to implement 
the Bank Protection Act of 1968, with which you enclosed a copy of 
your statement concerning this matter that was published in the 
Congressional Record for January 17, 1969. 
In particular, you ask that the Subcommittee be advised 
of the bases on which the decision was made by the Board not to 
require the utilization by banks under its supervision of more 
security devices than those that were made mandatory, and why no 
standards or requirements were promulgated for the use of steel 
bars or gratings for windows, skylights and doors, or for the 
separation of teller-stations from bank lobbies by bullet-resistant 
glass or other devices. 
Serious consideration was given to including a requirement 
for the use of steel bars or gratings on exterior windows and doors; 
indeed, the regulation as first proposed and published in the Federal 
Register for comment included such a requirement. It was concluded, 
however, that it is questionable whether these devices afford 
sufficient protection to warrant their cost and mandatory use in the 
case of banks. Anyone who is capable of equipping himself to attack 
a bank vault, the Board understands, would have no difficulty in 
equipping himself to defeat steel bars or gratings on windows and 
doors quite easily and quickly. Consideration was also given to 
requiring that conventional teller-stations be armored and the 
tellers isolated from the public; the regulation, as you know, states 
that walk-up and drive-in stations contracted for after February 15, 
1969, should be constructed so that tellers are effectively protected 

512 
The Honorable Dante B. Fascell 
-2-
by bullet-resistant barriers from persons outside the station. It 
was concluded, however, that a requirement that all teller stations 
be armored and the tellers isolated from the public would be in the 
nature of a maximum requirement rather than a "minimum" requirement, 
"reasonable in cost", as the Bank Protection Act prescribes. With 
the foregoing exceptions, the regulation includes virtually every 
security device the use of which was seriously advocated in the 
legislative history of the Act. 
As you point out, the Board's regulation allows bank manage-
ment considerable discretion in deciding what security devices to 
install. Placing the initial responsibility for such decisions on 
bank management is intended to accommodate the need for flexibility 
in the regulation, a need that is acknowledged in the legislative 
history of the Act. Flexibility is necessary, of course, because there 
are great differences in the extent to which banking offices are exposed 
to crime and, therefore, more security devices are appropriate for some 
banks than for others. But it should be understood that management's 
decisions as to what security devices to install are subject to review 
by the Board. The regulation requires each bank under the Board's 
supervision to file a statement of reasons in support of a decision not 
to install security devices at least equivalent to those described in 
Appendix A of the regulation. The regulation also provides that whenever 
the Board determines that the security devices or procedures used by a 
bank under its supervision are deficient, it may require the bank to 
take necessary corrective action. Thus, for example, a bank that 
believes it has good cause for not installing cameras may nevertheless 
be required to do so, if the Board disagrees with its decision. It is 
believed that this approach is more practicable from an administrative 
standpoint than the approach originally proposed under which a blanket 
requirement for security devices was coupled with a provision allowing 
banks to apply for waivers with respect to the devices. 
It should be stressed that the Board's regulation requires 
that each bank under its supervision file for each of its offices by 
March 15, 1969, and at such other times as the Board may require, a 
report on the security measures in effect, and information about past 
crimes against the office; in addition, each time a robbery, burglary 
or non-employee larceny is perpetrated or attempted at an office of a 
bank it must file a detailed report, including information on the 
effectiveness of security devices and procedures. These reports are 
designed to elicit information which, it is hoped, will enable the 
Board, in cooperation with the other interested Federal agencies, to 
make a better judgment as to what should be required in the way of
