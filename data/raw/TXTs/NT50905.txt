same-day substitution rule would serve primarily to upgrade accounts 
that were deeply undermargined in order to reduce their vulnerability 
to margin calls in the event of future stock market declines. 
During discussion at today's meeting Mr. Partee emphasized 
that he believed there would be less objection from the brokerage 
industry to this proposal than there had been to the 1959 proposal to 
remove the same-day substitution privilege. He based this belief in 
Part on the fact that margin requirements were much lower now than 
they were in 1959; also, the overall impact of the present proposal 
would be substantially less than that of the 1959 proposal, particu-
larly if the relatively low 40 per cent cut-off feature were adopted. 
The Board expressed general support for the staff proposal, 
and there was also alstrong feeling that over the coming months the 
Board should move in the direction of complete removal of the same-day 
substitution privilege. 
There was agreement that proper timing with respect to publica-
tion of the proposal was an important consideration and that it would 
desirable to publish the proposal for comment at a time when stock 
Prices were moving upward. In this regardnChairman Burns noted that 
the market had experienced declines for the previous two days and 
t^lat in his judgment today would not be a good time to act on the 
Proposal.

A question was then raised as to the appropriate per cent 
of equity below which the same-day substitution privilege would be 
withdrawn. It was suggested that rather than the AO per cent cut-
off proposed by the staff, an initial figure of 50 per cent might 
be used. It was pointed out, however, that a higher initial figure 
could be expected to create more resistance to the proposal. 
Chairman Burns noted,that he would prefer to proceed cautiously 
and to establish the initial figure at 40 per cent; this figure could 
be raised later if circumstances seemed to warrant such a move. He 
noted that the Board's factual evidence on the impact of the proposed

the period for comment appear to make it unwise to proceed with a 
higher cut-off, the Board would be left in an awkward position. Chairman 
Burns believed that another factor in favor of the lower 40 per cent 
figure was action that was scheduled to be taken soon by the Securities 
and Exchange Commission, which would require that sizable additions 
be made to the capital structure of brokerage firms. 
In a related comment Miss Hart expressed the belief that the 
brokerage industry would infer from this proposal that the Board 
intended to take additional steps over the coming months in the direc-
tion of more restraint on same-day substitution.

Dear Mr. Spiezio: 
This is in further response to your letter of January 11, 
1972, inquiring as to whether mortgage hanking companies which are 
subsidiaries of bank holding companies are subject to the Board's 
Statement on Civil Rights Act Nondiscrimination Requirements in 
Real Estate Loan Activities. 
As you know, the Department of Housing and Urban Develop-
ment, subsequent to the announcement of the Board's Statement, adopted 
Fair Housing Poster regulations which appear to be applicable to all 
persons subject to Title VIII of the Civil Rights Act of 1968. For 
the past few months, the Board's staff has been engaged in discussions 
with 11UD aimed at eliminating the overlapping coverage of these actions. 
These discussions have led to the granting by IIUD of a waiver from the 
HUD poster requirement for institutions subject to the jurisdiction of 
the Board which are required to adopt the poster requirements of Board's 
Statement as announced on December 17, 1971. 
In the light of the adoption by HUD of Fair Housing Poster 
regulations and the Board's desire to avoid unnecessary duplication 
°f regulatory and supervisory provisions in areas where, as in this case, 
another agency is given primary statutory responsibility, the Board 
has determined not to extend the application of its Statement at the 
Present, time to nonbank subsidiaries of bank holding companies that 
are engaged in making real estate loans. It is understood, however, 
that these companies will be subject to the HUD regulations (24 CFR, 
Part 110).

Dear Mr. Zucker: 
This refers to your letters of October 13 and 
November 30, 1971, and to our response of December 7, 1971. 
You ask that the Board of Governors consider amending its 
Regulation T, Credit by Brokers and Dealers, to extend from 
five to eight business days the time within which a broker 
must obtain from his customer the deposit of cash or secu-
rities required by the regulation as the result of a trans-
action in the customer's account. You state that in your 
experience, five business days barely allows sufficient 
time for the mailing of a confirmation to a customer and 
his return mailing of stock or cash. 
It should be pointed out initially, of course, 
that nothing in the Board's regulation forbids an exchange 
to adopt rules or regulations "further restricting the 
time or manner in which its members must obtain initial or 
additional margin in customer's accounts because of trans-
actions effected in such accounts" (§ 220.7(e)(1)). Thus, 
a change in the Board's rule would not necessarily result 
in a change in the rules of the New York or other stock 
exchanges, relating to the time for settlement. 
Because of the possible impact of an amendment 
extending the time for payment under Regulation T on the 
volume of "fails to deliver" and on back office problems

in the brokerage industry, the national securities exchange and 
a number of representative broker/dealer firms have been asked 
for their views on the advisability of the Board taking such 
action. After reviewing the matter in the light of the comments 
that have been received, the Board has determined that the possible 
detrimental effects of such a change, including increased exposure 
of brokerage firms to loss, increased volume of paper in the 
channels of communication relating to settlement of transactions, 
and the likelihood that customers would take advantage of the 
change to delay making payment, even where confirmations were 
received promptly, outweigh the administrative burden of obtaining 
and granting extensions of time in cases where required. Accord-
ingly, the Board has concluded that it would not presently be 
appropriate to amend Regulation T in this regard.
