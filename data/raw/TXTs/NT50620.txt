Federal Reserve offices in Florida. 
On the basis of a recently 
completed study of the need for additional Federal Reserve offices in 
Florida, the Federal Reserve Bank of Atlanta requested authorization to 
(1) retain the Jacksonville Branch; (2) open an additional branch in 
Miami to serve the banks in southern Florida, with interim installation 
of check collection and currency and coin service facilities; and (3) 
establish a currency and coin facility in the Tampa Bay area to serve 
the needs of central Florida. 
The Division of Federal Reserve Bank Operations in a memorandum 
dated April 22, 1970, recommended that the Board agree in principle to 
the establishment of a cash and check facility in the Miami area, but 
suggested that additional information was required to establish the need 
for a full branch at Miami and a facility in the Tampa Bay area. 
The Board's Committee on Organization, Compensation, and Build-
ing Plans met with President Kimbrel of the Federal Reserve Bank of 
Atlanta on May 15 and in a memorandum of May 18 recommended that the 
Board agree in principle to the Bank's first two proposals but not approve 
the establishment of a facility in the Tampa Bay area, since the need 
for such a facility was not sufficiently clear. 
Governor Mitchell began today's discussion by citing figures 
from a compilation that was handed to members of the Board showing, for 
standard metropolitan statistical areas with population of over 250,000, 
the number of banks, their total deposits, reserve city status, and

The tabulation also listed cities that had been the

A draft of press release was then distributed that would state 
that the Board had authorized the establishment of a check collection 
and currency and coin depot at Miami and that it had approved in prin-
ciple a plan by the Atlanta Reserve Bank to establish a branch at Miami 
but had fixed no time schedule, since future construction of branch bank 
buildings would require authorization from Congress increasing the 
$60 million limit on investments in Federal Reserve branch buildings. 
Mr. Cardon noted that this was not an auspicious time to request 
an increase in the $60 million limitation, and that announcement of 
approval of the establishment of a branch in Miami might reactivate cer-
tain earlier campaigns for additional branches in other cities. 
Governor Brimmer expressed the view that the wording of the 
draft press release did not accurately reflect the view of the Board's 
Committee. 
As he understood it, the Committee had concluded that the 
Board should at this time approve the eventual establishment of a branch 
in Miami. 
Therefore, he favored removal of the words "in principle". 
Governor Robertson suggested that only a currency and check 
facility be approved at this time, on the theory that the actual operation

Governor Brimmer then commented on attitudes in the Miami area, 
noting in particular that First National Bank of Miami, which had been 
acting as a clearing bank, now proposed to charge a fee for such services 
and that the Miami Chamber of Commerce had expressed itself in favor of 
a Federal Reserve branch in Miami. 
Governor Brimmer also reported a 
view by Atlanta Reserve Bank officials that Miami area bankers would con-
strue the providing of a facility rather than a branch as second-class 
treatment, and difficult bank relations problems would be likely to ensue. 
He cited information from a letter just received from President Kimbrel 
summarizing the major points in favor of a full branch and the estimated 
comparative operating costs of a branch and a facility, along with related 
reductions in the cost of operating the Jacksonville Branch. 
It was 
understood that, as requested by Governor Brimmer, the letter would be 
placed in the record of today's meeting. 
Further discussion emphasized that it would be undesirable to imply 
that the need for action by Congress was the immediate obstacle to estab-
lishment of a Miami branch. 
Even if the branch were approved, the devel-
opment of plans would take some time. 
Comments were made on the possibility 
of leasing branch quarters, or otherwise operating the office without con-
structing a building, and it was noted that a branch initially might not 
offer all of the usual services.

First Wisconsin Bankshares. 
On May 6, 1970, the Board denied 
the application of First Wisconsin Bankshares Corporation, Milwaukee, 
Wisconsin, to acquire shares of First West Towne National Bank of 
Madison, Madison, Wisconsin, a proposed new bank. 
It was agreed, in 
view of the 3-2 vote, that a request for reconsideration might appropri-
ately be made by a member of the Board to permit all Board members to 
participate. 
Such a motion was subsequently made by Governor Robertson 
and approved. 
Governor Mitchell now displayed maps and photographs relating 
to the locations of the banking offices in the area. 
Reference to these 
documents, he said, had not changed his view that the application should 
be approved. 
The point at issue was whether a well-established banking 
organization should be permitted to establish a subsidiary in this devel-
oping area, or whether the site should be reserved for another bank. 
In his view, the prospective growth of the area would support not only 
the bank now proposed but other entrants. 
Governor Daane supported the application for the reasons Governor 
Mitchell had outlined and also on the grounds of convenience and needs. 
He did not think it proper to deny the application simply against the

-7-
possibility that another new bank would be chartered for the location 
when there were no charters pending, although a new bank under different 
auspices might have been preferable in the first instance. 
Governor Maisel was persuaded by the adverse positions taken 
by the Federal Reserve Bank of Chicago and by the State Commissioner of 
Banking. 
In his view, the Bank Holding Company Act was directed toward 
giving States a strong voice on what kind of banking structure they 
wanted. 
The State Commissioner had referred to the fact that here the 
applicant controlled 39 per cent of deposits in the Madison area, and 
had indicated an expectation that other applicants would seek entry. 
The Commissioner preferred the entry of a different new bank both for 
improvement of competition and for reduction of concentration. 
Governor Brimmer shared Governor Maisel's view. 
He recalled 
that the Board had in 1968 denied an application by this same holding 
company in somewhat similar circumstances, and it also seemed to him 
that in this instance the degree of market concentration was already high 
enough to justify denial. 
It appeared to Governor Sherrill that the principal reason for 
denial was the hope that another organization would seek entry, yet the 
Madison area already had a large number of banks. 
The competition seemed 
quite viable. 
It had been contended that there were no needs now unserved 
however, that was a point of judgment, and he thought it was a marginal 
decision.

In a move to improve banking services in the rapidly expanding 
southern Florida area, the Board of Governors of the Federal Reserve 
System announced today it has authorized the Federal Reserve Bank of 
Atlanta to establish a check collection and currency and coin facility 
at Miami. 
At the same time, the Board approved a proposal by the Atlanta 
Reserve Bank to plan the establishment of a branch bank at Miami. 
The check collection and currency and coin facility is expected 
to begin operations by the end of the year. 
It will provide services in 
13 southern Florida counties as follows: 
Broward, Charlotte, Collier, Dade, 
Glades, Hendry, Indian River, Lee, Martin, Monroe, Okeechobee, Palm Beach 
and St. Lucie. 
Growth in Florida's economy has been concentrated in the southern 
part of the State which has growing trade and other economic relationships 
with Central and South America. 
There are now about 150 commercial banks, 
including 86 member banks, in the 13 counties that will be served by a 
Miami facility. 
These banks account for 44 per cent of the total bank 
deposits in the State and receive about 52 per cent of the dollar value of 
currency shipped by the Jacksonville Branch of the Fed eral Reserve Bank of 
Atlanta, which now serves southern Florida. 
Estimates indicate that the proposed Miami facility will handle 
about 14 million checks monthly, a volume that would be exceeded to any

The 
establishment of a Miami office would lead to substantial improvement in 
Federal Reserve services in that area and would also relieve pressures on 
the Jacksonville Branch where space problems could become so severe within 
three to five years that construction of a new building would be required 
if that Branch were to continue serving the entire State. 
The 330 miles from Jacksonville to Miami represents the greatest 
distance by far between a Federal Reserve, office and a metropolitan area of 
more than one million persons. 
The 1968 population of the Miami metropolitan 
area was an estimated 1.12 million persons.

Dear Mr. Kimbrel: 
This refers to your letter of March 24, 1970, transmitting 
to the Board a report entitled "The Florida Project" and requesting 
that the Board grant approval to the Federal Reserve Bank of Atlanta 
to implement the recommendations in the report as soon as practicable. 
The recommendations referred to were that your Bank be permitted to :' 
"Retain its Jacksonville Branch; 
"Open an additional Branch in the City of Miami to 
serve the banks in southern Florida; and until this 
can be accomplished, install in Miami check collec-
tion and currency and coin service facilities to 
serve this area for the interim period; 
"Establish a currency and coin facility in the 
Tampa Bay area to serve the needs of banks in 
Central Florida." 
In the light of this report and of further discussions of 
the matter between representatives of your Bank and the Board's 
Committee on Organization, Compensation, and Building Plans, the 
Board hereby authorizes the Federal Reserve Bank of Atlanta (a) to 
establish a check collection and currency and coin facility in the 
Miami area, and (b) to plan the establishment of a Branch Bank at 
Miami. 
Underlying this action was an understanding that the 
Jacksonville Branch would continue to serve the northern and central 
parts of Florida, and that the proposal for a facility in the Tampa 
Bay area would be held in abeyance.

Dear Governor Brimmer: 
We understand that consideration of the Florida proposal has 
been deferred until a future meeting of the full Board. 
This being the 
case, wa have taken advantage of the additional time to restate the case 
for a full branch vis-a-vis a check-money facility. 
The following major 
points are pertinent: 
1. 
In view of vault requirements, provision of space 
for currency and coin is very expensive relative 
to the rest of the building. 
In addition, a check-
money facility would require many of the support 
functions that a full branch would entail. 
The 
cost of personnel and of space involved in adding 
the necessary functions for a full branch, therefore, 
would be considerably below the average cost of 
personnel and space for a check-money facility alone. 
2. 
The additional functions (accounting and fiscal), 
however, would allow us to expand materially the 
services offered banks and the general public. 
A 
considerable saving would be realized through a 
reduction in cost of some operations at Jacksonville. 
On balance, we believe that the value of such 
additional services will more than offset the extra 
cost. As noted on the enclosed schedule, we estimate 
the additional annual cost of operations to be 
approximately $700,000. 
3. 
It would be very difficult to explain to the Miami-
area banks the reasons for providing a check-money
